# Case Study: Phishing Attack

## Overview
Phishing is a cybercrime where fraudsters disguise 
themselves as trustworthy organisations to steal 
sensitive information like passwords, OTPs, and 
banking details.

## Real Incident
In 2020, a large-scale phishing attack targeted 
students and faculty of several Indian universities. 
Victims received emails that appeared to come from 
their university's official IT department. The email 
stated that their university login credentials would 
expire within 24 hours and asked them to click a 
link to renew them immediately.

The link led to a fake website that looked identical 
to the university portal. Students who entered their 
username and password unknowingly handed their login 
credentials directly to the attackers. The fraudsters 
then used these credentials to access university 
systems, steal personal data, and in some cases 
access linked payment portals.

## How It Happened (Step by Step)
1. Victim receives an official-looking email from 
   a fake university IT department address.
2. Email creates urgency — "Your account expires 
   in 24 hours. Click here to renew."
3. Victim clicks the link and lands on a fake 
   website that looks exactly like the real portal.
4. Victim enters their username and password.
5. Credentials are instantly captured by the attacker.
6. Attacker logs into the real university portal 
   using stolen credentials.
7. Personal data, assignment submissions, and 
   linked financial accounts are compromised.

## Who Is Usually Targeted
- First-year college students who are new to 
  institutional email systems
- Faculty members with access to sensitive 
  university data
- Students who have linked their bank accounts 
  to university fee portals
- Anyone who responds quickly to urgent-sounding 
  official emails

## Consequences
- Loss of access to university accounts
- Personal and academic data stolen
- Financial loss if banking details were linked
- Identity theft using stolen credentials
- Psychological stress and anxiety
- In severe cases, academic records were 
  tampered with

## What Could Have Been Done Differently
- Always check the sender's actual email address 
  — not just the display name
- Never click links in emails directly — instead 
  type the website URL manually in the browser
- The university should have used email 
  authentication protocols like DMARC and SPF
- Students should have been trained to identify 
  phishing emails during orientation
